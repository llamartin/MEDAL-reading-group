Energy Markets & Electricity
Nuclear Operations with a High Penetration of Renewables: The Case of France
Nicolas Astier & Frank A. Wolak
NBER WP 2024
Nuclear and intermittent renewables are generally regarded as the only scalable technologies producing low-carbon electricity. Using hourly data from the French power system, this paper finds that nuclear units are operated quite flexibly, but that additional load following events are associated with a higher likelihood of unit failure. In 2024, hours during which available nuclear flexibility was exhausted are associated with non-positive hourly day-ahead prices.

Power Flows: Transmission Lines, Allocative Efficiency, and Corporate Profits
Catherine Hausman
AER 2025
Accelerated investment in electricity transmission could reduce total costs and enhance renewable integration. This paper documents static allocative inefficiencies induced by incomplete market integration in two major US markets — totalling $2 billion in 2022. It also shows that 4 firms would have experienced a collective $1.3 billion drop in net revenues had the market been integrated, suggesting incumbent firms may have incentives to block transmission projects.

Power Flows, Part 2: Transmission Lowers US Generation Costs, But Generator Incentives Are Not Aligned
Dasom Ham, Owen Kay & Catherine Hausman
RFF WP 2025
Eliminating interregional transmission constraints would have reduced US generation costs by $5.8–7.1 billion in 2022 and $3.4–5.0 billion in 2023. Despite these system-wide savings, market integration creates clear winners and losers among generation companies — producers in some regions have incentives to delay or block grid integration despite overall benefits.

Does Regulation Distort Exit Decisions? Evidence from U.S. Power Plants
Lucas W. Davis & Paige E. Weber
NBER WP 2024
Using generator-level data from 2010–2023, this paper shows that regulated power plant units have been 45% less likely to exit than unregulated units. For unregulated units, exit decisions follow wholesale electricity prices and capital costs. Owners of regulated units are largely insulated from these factors and in some cases have strong incentives to continue operating capital-intensive equipment beyond its economic life.

AI in Charge: Large-Scale Experimental Evidence on Electric Vehicle Charging Demand
Robert D. Metcalfe, Andrew Schein, Cohen R. Simpson & Yixin Sun
NBER WP 2025
A large-scale natural field experiment evaluating an AI-managed EV charging tariff finds a 42% reduction in household electricity demand during peak hours, with 100% shifted to lower-cost, lower-carbon periods. Algorithm overrides were low, consumer savings were substantial, and results were consistent across the UK, US, Germany, and Spain — suggesting significant scalable welfare gains from AI-managed charging.

Choosing Who Chooses: Selection-Driven Targeting in Energy Rebate Programs
Takanori Ida, Takunori Ishihara, Koichiro Ito, Daido Kido, Toru Kitagawa, Shosei Sakaguchi & Shusaku Sasaki
Forthcoming Econometrica
We develop an optimal policy assignment rule that integrates two distinctive approaches commonly used in economics — targeting by observables and targeting through self-selection. Our method can be used with experimental or quasi-experimental data to identify who should be treated, be untreated, and self-select to achieve a policymaker's objective. Applying this method to a randomized controlled trial on a residential energy rebate program, we find that targeting that optimally exploits both observable data and self-selection outperforms conventional targeting. We use the Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE) framework (Imbens and Angrist (1994)) to investigate the mechanism in our approach. By estimating several key LATEs based on the random variation created by our experiment, we demonstrate how our method allows policymakers to identify whose self-selection would be valuable and harmful to social welfare.

Voltage Quality and Economic Activity
S. Berkouwer, P. Biscaye, M. Mikdash, S. Puller & C. Wolfram
R&R JPE: Micro
Novel minute-by-minute customer-level power measurements in urban Ghana reveal systematic under-voltage, damaging equipment and incentivising protective investments. Customers would pay 10% more for electricity with better voltage. Quasi-random grid improvements raised average voltage by 5.5V, reducing appliance damages, but yielded minimal economic impacts after one year — pointing to complexity in allocating long-term grid investments using short-term data.

Holding Up Green Energy: Counterparty Risk in the Indian Solar Power Market
Nicholas Ryan
Cond. Accept Econometrica
Counterparty risk of an average Indian state increases solar energy prices by 10%. Central government intermediation eliminates this risk premium entirely. Higher prices reduce investment because state demand for green energy is elastic. Results suggest that the risk of hold-up places developing countries at a structural disadvantage in the procurement of green energy.

Environmental Policy & Regulation
Can Pollution Markets Work in Developing Countries? Experimental Evidence from India
Michael Greenstone, Rohini Pande, Nicholas Ryan & Anant Sudarshan
QJE 2025
The world's first particulate matter emissions market, covering industrial plants in a large Indian city, achieved 99% permit compliance vs. 66% under command-and-control. Treatment plants reduced emissions by 20–30% and abatement costs fell by an estimated 11%. Mortality benefits exceeded costs by at least twenty-five times. The market functioned well even in a low-capacity institutional environment.

Regulating Untaxable Externalities: Are Vehicle Air Pollution Standards Effective and Efficient?
Mark R. Jacobsen, James M. Sallee, Joseph S. Shapiro & Arthur van Benthem
QJE 2023
Air pollution emissions per mile of new US vehicles fell by over 99% since exhaust standards began in 1967, with standards causing most of this decline. Yet standards are not cost-effective because they fail to encourage scrap of older vehicles, which account for the majority of emissions. Increasing registration fees on dirty vehicles would yield even larger welfare gains by accelerating scrap, though with complex distributional effects.
Link
Carbon Taxation and Firm Behavior in Emerging Economies: Evidence from South Africa
Johannes Gallé, Rodrigo Oliveira, Daniel Overbeck, Nadine Riedel & Edson R. Severnini
NBER WP 2025
The first comprehensive evidence on firm responses to carbon taxation in an emerging economy. South Africa's 2019 carbon tax had no negative effects on firm performance or jobs. Firms facing higher effective tax rates actually increased activity following the announcement — four years before implementation — likely reflecting resolution of regulatory uncertainty and efforts to mitigate stranded asset costs.

The Geographical Leakage of Environmental Regulation: Evidence from the Clean Air Act
E. Mark Curtis, Weiting Miao, Felix Samy Soliman, Juan Carlos Suárez Serrato & Daniel Yi Xu
Working Paper 2025
Approximately 40% of geographic leakage from Clean Air Act Amendments is driven by within-firm reallocation across locations — highlighting the critical role of multi-unit firms in shifting economic activity across regions. A spatial equilibrium model integrating these findings quantifies the economic cost of the regulation and its role in redistributing US industrial production.

Downwind and Out: The Strategic Dispersion of Power Plants and Their Pollution
John Morehouse & Edward Rubin
Forthcoming JAERE
Decision-makers disproportionately sited coal-fueled power plants to reduce counties' and states' own downwind pollution exposure. Natural-gas plants — lower polluters — did not follow this strategy. Within 6 hours, 50% of coal plants' emissions leave their source states and 99% depart source counties — illustrating how local strategic responses challenge federalist environmental governance.

Water, Land & Natural Resources
Rationing the Commons
Nicholas Ryan & Anant Sudarshan
JPE 2022
Rationing agricultural groundwater in Rajasthan, India leads farmers to use roughly the socially optimal amount of water on average — yet the regime is grossly inefficient because it misallocates water across farmers, lowering productivity. Pigouvian reform would increase agricultural surplus by 12% of household income yet still fall well short of a Pareto improvement over rationing.
Link
The Value of Clean Water: Experimental Evidence from Rural India
Fiona Burlig, Amir Jina & Anant Sudarshan
Forthcoming AER
A 60,000-household cluster RCT testing decentralised treatment and home-delivery of clean water finds take-up exceeding 90% at low prices. Willingness-to-pay is several times higher than prior indirect estimates; willingness-to-accept exceeds marginal cost. Self-reported health measures improve accordingly, and free water delivery regimes appear highly cost-effective on a cost-per-DALY basis.
Link
Conservation Priorities and Environmental Offsets: Markets for Florida Wetlands
Daniel Aronoff & Will Rafey
Working Paper
Wetland offset markets in Florida generated $2.4 billion of net surplus from 1995–2020 relative to direct conservation. However, offset trading also generated new hydrological externalities. A locally differentiated Pigouvian tax would have prevented $1.6 billion of new flood damage while preserving more than two-thirds of the private gains from trade.

Machine Learning Predicts Which Rivers, Streams, and Wetlands the Clean Water Act Regulates
Simon Greenhill, Hannah Druckenmiller, Sherrie Wang, David A. Keiser, et al.
Science 2025
A deep learning model trained on 150,000 Army Corps jurisdictional determinations reveals that a 2020 White House rule deregulated 690,000 stream miles, 35 million wetland acres, and 30% of waters around drinking-water sources. The framework can support permitting, policy design, and use of machine learning in regulatory implementation problems.

Consequences of Land Use Regulation Under the Clean Water Act
Hannah Druckenmiller, Joseph S. Shapiro & Charles A. Taylor
Working Paper
Recent White House and Supreme Court rule changes greatly altered Clean Water Act regulatory stringency, measured using geophysical data and machine learning. Regulation substantially decreases values of non-residential land parcels with water resources and decreases development activity, as measured from permitting and satellite data.

Climate, Health & Behaviour
Mandated versus Voluntary Adaptation to Natural Disasters: The Case of US Wildfires
Patrick Baylis & Judson Boomhower
JPE 2026
California wildfire building codes increase structure survival and reduce structure-to-structure spread. The paper develops and estimates a model comparing the social benefits of mandatory building standards versus voluntary adaptation policies — finding remarkable resilience effects and positive spillovers to neighbouring structures from mandated codes.

How Are Insurance Markets Adapting to Climate Change? Risk Classification and Pricing in Homeowners Insurance
Judson Boomhower, Meredith Fowlie, Jacob Gellman & Andrew Plantinga
NBER WP 2024
Firms relying on coarser wildfire risk measures face severe adverse selection and charge higher prices in high-risk segments — or exit these markets entirely. A theoretical model incorporating asymmetric risk classification and market regulation explains the empirical patterns: the winner's curse can increase prices and limit participation in insurance markets for large, hard-to-model risks.

Misperceptions About Air Pollution: Implications for Willingness to Pay and Environmental Inequality
Matthew Tarduno & Reed Walker
Working Paper
US respondents overestimate both their air pollution exposure and its impact on life expectancy. Using randomised information on actual air pollution, the paper traces how experimentally induced belief changes affect WTP for air quality. Despite some differences in misperceptions between Black and White respondents, rectifying these misperceptions would not meaningfully close observed gaps in WTP or pollution exposure.

Impacts of Contemporaneous Air Pollution Exposure on Cognitive Performance in Kenya
Xuqian Ma, Michelle N. Layvant, Edward Miguel, Eric Ochieng, Ajay Pillarisetti & Michael W. Walker
NBER WP 2025
Higher PM2.5 exposure during cognitive testing in Kenya is associated with lower performance in memory, executive function, and visuospatial tasks. Effects are significantly larger among more educated individuals and are evident at PM2.5 levels below Kenya's regulatory threshold — suggesting short-term exposure imposes underappreciated human capital costs that current standards fail to mitigate.

Trade, Finance & Behaviour
Institutions, Comparative Advantage, and the Environment
Joseph S. Shapiro
ReStud 2025
Strong institutions — financial, judicial, and labour market — provide comparative advantage in clean industries, and thereby improve a country's environmental quality. Five complementary tests evaluate this hypothesis. A quantitative general equilibrium model indicates that strengthening institutions decreases domestic pollution by relocating dirty industries abroad, though it increases pollution in other countries.

Trade, Trees, and Lives
Xinming Du, Lei Li & Eric Zou
NBER WP 2024
Brazilian agricultural export shocks cause a near one-for-one decline in forest cover. Loss of forests that filter air pollutants as they travel causes downstream rises in air pollution and mortality. Estimated mortality effects: over 700,000 premature deaths in Brazil over two decades, equating to $0.18 loss in statistical life value per $1 agricultural exports.

The Effects of Regulating Greenwashing: Evidence from Europe's Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation
Hunt Allcott, Mark L. Egan, Paul Smeets & Hanbin Yang
NBER WP 2025
The EU's SFDR had little effect on fund flows or portfolio sustainability. Disclosures were ineffective because they offered little new information beyond what investors could already infer from fund names and mandates. Experimental evidence shows current disclosures have minimal impact on investor decisions, but more intuitive presentation of the information could substantially improve the regulation's effectiveness.

ESG Aversion: Experimental Evidence on Perceptions and Preferences
Ye Zhang & Eric Zou
NBER WP 2024
ESG-labelled startup profiles receive lower interest from both founders and VCs — primarily driven by negative performance beliefs. A willingness-to-pay experiment reveals a latent preference for ESG once performance concerns are held constant. These findings highlight a tension between financial returns and personal values: profitability concerns obscure an underlying taste for ESG.

Self- and Social Signaling: Evidence from Solar Adoption in California
Bollinger, Gillingham & Kirkpatrick
NBER WP 2025
Self-signaling is separately identified from social signaling in residential solar adoption by exploiting exogenous variation in the visibility of potential solar arrays. Political affiliation of proximate peers influences the extent of self-signaling and is crowded out by the private benefits of installing solar — distinguishing signalling motivations from warm glow and other prosocial explanations.

Equity & Distributional Impacts
The Distribution of Power: Favoritism, Efficiency, and Equity in Energy Infrastructure
Catherine D. Wolfram, Susanna B. Berkouwer, Felipe Vial, Eric Hsu, Oliver W. Kim & Edward Miguel
Working Paper
Areas with pro-government vote shares in Kenya received 35–42% more household electrification than the legislated formula stipulated. Surprisingly, this deviation increased total consumer surplus by 2% — at the expense of opposition voters, who received 21% less surplus gain than under the formula. The counterfactual surplus-maximising allocation is in fact politically neutral, meaning political bias cannot be justified on efficiency grounds alone.

Who Bears the Burden of Climate Inaction?
Kimberly A. Clausing, Christopher R. Knittel & Catherine Wolfram
NBER WP 2025
An aggregate annual climate cost averaging between $400 and $900 per US household, with costs exceeding $1,300 in 10% of counties. Costs disproportionately burden lower-income households. Natural disasters — not rising temperatures — account for the bulk of the current burden, emphasising the importance of research beyond temperature effects.
